History of Cofffee

Coffee was first discovered in Ethiopia, where a goat herder named Kaldi noticed his goats became energetic after eating coffee berries. By the 15th century,
it spread to Yemen and the Arab world, becoming popular in Sufi religious practices and public coffee houses. In the 1600s, it reached Europe, where it
was initially controversial but quickly gained popularity. European powers then spread coffee cultivation globally — to Java, the Caribbean, and Brazil,
which became the top producer. In the U.S., coffee became especially popular after the Boston Tea Party. Today, supported by organizations like the
National Coffee Association (founded in 1911), coffee is a global drink with growing focus on specialty quality, sustainability, and ethical sourcing.

Rise of Coffee Plantation in Asia.

The rise of coffee began in ancient Ethiopia with its discovery by a goat herder named Kaldi, and quickly spread across the Arab world, where it became central to
religious and social life. By the 15th century, coffee was being cultivated in Yemen and served in public coffee houses, becoming a vital part of Islamic culture.
In the 17th century, coffee entered Europe and grew in popularity, despite initial resistance. Coffee houses became centers of intellectual exchange and commerce.
As global demand soared, colonial powers spread coffee cultivation to Asia, Africa, and Latin America. In the United States, coffee gained patriotic significance
after the Boston Tea Party and became a national favorite. Over time, coffee evolved from a simple drink into a global industry, symbolizing energy, conversation,
and culture — with modern trends focusing on specialty brews, fair trade, and sustainability.

Rise of Coffee in Asia.

The rise of coffee in Asia began in the 17th century through trade and colonial influence. Initially introduced by European colonizers and traders, coffee cultivation
spread to countries like India, Indonesia,and Sri Lanka. Over time, local farmers adopted coffee farming, especially in highland regions. In recent decades,
Asian countries like Vietnam (now one of the world’s top coffee producers), Thailand, and China have developed strong coffee cultures and industries,
blending traditional tastes with global trends, making Asia a major player in both coffee production and consumption today.

Coffee Replaces Tea in the New World

In the New World, especially in colonial America, coffee began replacing tea after the Boston Tea Party in 1773, when American colonists protested British taxation
by dumping tea into Boston Harbor. This event sparked a patriotic shift—drinking coffee became a symbol of American independence and resistance against
British rule. As a result, many Americans turned to coffee as their preferred beverage. Over time, coffee grew in popularity, supported by increased imports
from the Caribbean and Latin America, eventually becoming the dominant drink in the United States and across much of the New World.
